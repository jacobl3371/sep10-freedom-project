Compartmentalizing Your Identity From Your Online Data

Context

When accessing all of the day to day services and viewing information on the web I believe that it is essential –and have found for myself that it eliminates such intrusions as spam and other data mining– to use Tor browser, which utilizes the Tor anonymity network which allows the user to access websites without them seeing the user's real IP address and subsequently, location by routing all traffic over the Tor network, and uses isolation features built into the browser to isolate all browser activity from the rest of the activity on the operating system. Many educational services which students must use throughout their lives such as the DOE-gatewayed, nycstudents.net Google Classroom login, and even the non-DOE Google Classroom block the only software, which by default is the only software that completely anonymizes the user's internet traffic within an amnesic and isolationary browser: Tor, and its decentralized relay enabled IP addresses which the user assumes once they attempt to enter a website.

Addressing The Issue

My topic is about how to facilitate the most compartmentalized, and pseudonymous access to your education, this being something that the infrastructure of public education in the ever growing digital age is lacking. The problem that I hope to solve with my project is that many educational services block access to their websites through Tor browser.

Identifying My Goal

Perhaps, if sites block access through the Tor network, accessing them through Tor browser would require the use of a different network. This way, my goal is to make educational services that block the Tor network accessible through Tor browser by configuring an installation of the browser to route all of its traffic through a VPN service instead of the Tor network.

Software used to facilitate online education:

Currently, if you try to access the nycstudents.net Google classroom login through Tor browser, you will see this error message, saying that the service is unavailable since the DOE servers detected the use of tor exit nodes masking the IP address of the user requesting the website from the server, and they take the liberty to make sure that no one anonymizing their traffic through Tor in Tor browser could ever use their services. Most people would give up and use a regular browser after this, but I wanted to see how much agency a K-12 students relying on digital services facilitated by Google and the DOE for access to their education could actually acquire.

Even the regular classroom.google.com url blocks the Tor network when you attempt to enter it from Tor browser. 9 out of 10 times when you type in the url into Tor browser and click enter, you will be prompted with this 403 error URL declinement message.

The other tenth of the time, you may get to the login but will be prompted with a message saying that Google's services are not available in your country. This is the error I am usually prompted with when trying to access Google Classroom through Tor via Google drive or Docs to get around the classroom.google.com url block. On a seldom occassion it may work and I am able to access regular Google Classroom services (hstat.org login) through Tor in Tor browser, but most of the time you will be prompted with this error. I didn't understand it at first since Tor exit relays origniate from countries all over Europe and North America, so I couldn't see how Google could block their services for those IP addresses. Only after I clicked the 'learn more' button I realized that Google was just trying to facilitate complete hostility to all IP addresses detected as Tor exit relays, most being blocked at this point, and justifying the denial of their Google workspace services to Tor users under the guise that their service can not be distributed to the totalitarian regime which the Tor user who is entering the website from the United States behind a Netherlands or France-run exit relay supposedly lives under.

Google lists some of the dictatorships that they restrict their business services to like Russia, Cuba, Russian-occupied Ukrainian territories like Luhansk and Donetsk, Iran, North Korea and Syria. Most people in these regions don't even have internet access, let alone are accessing Google workspace services in these countries with no educational system or economy. Google saw that they could kill two birds with one stone by blocking their workspace services in these underdeveloped regimes which are all currently at war, either more directly or not, with the West, and cripple Tor user's ability to compartmentalize their identity on google classroom in Tor Browser from the rest of the data on their operating system and devices, basically saying that people who want to securely and pseudononymously access Google's workspace services through the use of the only free open and source software that does this; Tor and Tor browser, should be given as little capability to do so as someone living without internet in a war-torn regime.
